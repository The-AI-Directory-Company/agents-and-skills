# Retrospective Facilitation Formats

Four formats with instructions, timing, and guidance on when to use each one. Rotate formats every 3-4 sprints to prevent staleness.

---

## 1. Start / Stop / Continue

### When to Use

- Teams new to retrospectives
- Sprints where things were generally stable with incremental improvements needed
- When the team needs a simple, low-overhead format

### How to Run

**Total time: 45 minutes**

| Phase | Time | Activity |
|-------|------|----------|
| Setup | 2 min | Draw three columns on the board: Start, Stop, Continue |
| Silent writing | 5 min | Each person writes observations on sticky notes — one per note |
| Share and cluster | 10 min | Each person places notes in the appropriate column; facilitator groups related items |
| Discuss | 15 min | Pick top 2-3 clusters by dot voting; discuss root causes |
| Actions | 10 min | Convert top themes into experiments with owners |
| Close | 3 min | Read back action items; confirm owners and timelines |

### Column Definitions

| Column | Question | Example |
|--------|----------|---------|
| **Start** | What should we begin doing that we are not doing today? | "Start writing acceptance criteria before sprint planning" |
| **Stop** | What should we stop doing because it is not working? | "Stop scheduling meetings during focus time blocks" |
| **Continue** | What is working well and should not change? | "Continue the 15-minute deploy verification after each release" |

### Facilitation Tips

- If the "Continue" column is empty, the team may be in a negativity spiral. Prompt: "What is one thing that worked this sprint that you want to protect?"
- If "Start" and "Stop" are mirrors of each other ("Start writing tests" / "Stop shipping without tests"), consolidate them — they are the same observation.

## 2. 4Ls (Liked, Learned, Lacked, Longed For)

### When to Use

- After a sprint with significant learning moments (new technology, new team member, new process)
- When the team needs to reflect on growth, not just process
- When Start/Stop/Continue feels stale

### How to Run

**Total time: 50 minutes**

| Phase | Time | Activity |
|-------|------|----------|
| Setup | 2 min | Draw four quadrants: Liked, Learned, Lacked, Longed For |
| Silent writing | 5 min | One observation per sticky note, placed in the matching quadrant |
| Read-aloud | 8 min | Facilitator reads each note aloud; author can add 10-second context |
| Cluster | 5 min | Group related items across quadrants (a "Lacked" and a "Longed For" often pair) |
| Discuss | 15 min | Dot vote on clusters; discuss top 2-3 |
| Actions | 12 min | Convert into experiments |
| Close | 3 min | Read back actions |

### Quadrant Definitions

| Quadrant | Question | Emphasis |
|----------|----------|----------|
| **Liked** | What did you enjoy or appreciate this sprint? | Positive reinforcement — what to protect |
| **Learned** | What did you discover or realize? | New knowledge — capture and share |
| **Lacked** | What was missing that would have helped? | Gaps in tools, process, information, or support |
| **Longed For** | What do you wish existed or happened? | Aspirational — desired future state |

### Facilitation Tips

- "Learned" often surfaces undocumented knowledge. Capture these as documentation tasks if they would help others.
- "Longed For" items that appear sprint after sprint are candidates for roadmap investment, not just retro action items.

## 3. Mad / Sad / Glad

### When to Use

- When there is visible emotional tension or low morale
- After a difficult sprint (missed deadline, incident, team conflict)
- When the team needs permission to express frustration before problem-solving

### How to Run

**Total time: 50 minutes**

| Phase | Time | Activity |
|-------|------|----------|
| Setup | 2 min | Draw three columns: Mad, Sad, Glad |
| Context setting | 3 min | Facilitator: "This format starts with how we feel. All emotions are valid. We will move to actions after we understand the landscape." |
| Silent writing | 5 min | One observation per note — can be emotional ("frustrated by...") or factual |
| Share | 10 min | Each person shares their notes. No rebuttals or problem-solving during sharing. |
| Cluster | 5 min | Group related items |
| Discuss | 15 min | Focus on: "What is driving this feeling? What can we change?" |
| Actions | 8 min | Convert into experiments — focus on what is within the team's control |
| Close | 2 min | Acknowledge the emotions; read back actions |

### Column Definitions

| Column | Emotion | Example |
|--------|---------|---------|
| **Mad** | Frustration, anger, irritation | "Mad that we committed to a deadline without being consulted" |
| **Sad** | Disappointment, loss, regret | "Sad that the feature we worked on for 3 sprints was deprioritized" |
| **Glad** | Happiness, relief, pride | "Glad the team rallied during the incident and resolved it in 2 hours" |

### Facilitation Tips

- This format requires stronger facilitation. Do not let "Mad" items turn into blame sessions. Redirect: "What can we, as a team, change about this?"
- If the "Glad" column is empty, the team is in trouble. Spend extra time here — even small wins matter.
- Consider having an external facilitator (scrum master from another team, manager who is not part of the retro) when tensions are high.

## 4. Timeline

### When to Use

- After a complex sprint with many events (incident, scope change, launch, team change)
- When the team needs to reconstruct what actually happened before drawing conclusions
- When patterns across time are more important than categories

### How to Run

**Total time: 55 minutes**

| Phase | Time | Activity |
|-------|------|----------|
| Setup | 3 min | Draw a horizontal timeline spanning the sprint dates. Mark the sprint start, mid-point, and end. Add known events (deploy dates, incident, scope change). |
| Silent annotation | 7 min | Each person adds sticky notes to the timeline at the approximate date. Include: events, observations, energy levels (high/low), and turning points. |
| Walk the timeline | 10 min | Facilitator walks through the timeline chronologically. Ask: "What was happening here? How was the team feeling?" |
| Identify patterns | 10 min | Look for: energy dips, clusters of problems, cause-and-effect chains, turning points |
| Discuss | 15 min | Focus on 2-3 patterns. Ask: "Was this preventable? What would we do differently?" |
| Actions | 8 min | Convert patterns into experiments |
| Close | 2 min | Read back actions |

### Facilitation Tips

- The timeline makes cause-and-effect visible. "We got the scope change on Tuesday, and by Thursday the team was working overtime" is a powerful observation that category-based formats might miss.
- Use colored dots or stickers for energy levels: green (high energy, good), yellow (neutral), red (low energy, struggling).
- If the team cannot remember what happened in the first week, that itself is a data point — the sprint was so chaotic that early work was overshadowed.

## Format Selection Guide

| Situation | Recommended Format |
|-----------|--------------------|
| First retro with a new team | Start/Stop/Continue |
| Stable sprint, incremental improvement | Start/Stop/Continue |
| Sprint with significant learning | 4Ls |
| Low morale or visible frustration | Mad/Sad/Glad |
| Complex sprint with many events | Timeline |
| Team has used the same format 3x in a row | Any different format |
| Post-incident sprint | Mad/Sad/Glad or Timeline |
| Sprint after a major launch | 4Ls or Timeline |

### Rotation Schedule

Track which format was used each sprint. If the same format is used 3 times consecutively, switch regardless of circumstances. Repetition causes autopilot — the team stops surfacing new observations because the format no longer prompts fresh thinking.
