# Product Launch Brief

## Before you start

Gather the following from the user. Items 1-4 are required before proceeding:

1. **What is being launched?** Feature, product, major update, or pricing change. Be specific about scope — "launching AI features" is too broad.
2. **Who is the target audience?** Primary and secondary segments. Include their current behavior and what needs to change.
3. **Why should the audience care?** The core value proposition in one sentence. If you cannot say it in one sentence, the positioning is not ready.
4. **When is the launch date?** Fixed or flexible? Are there external dependencies (conference, partner launch, regulatory deadline)?
5. **What tier is this launch?** Tier 1 (major — press, event, full campaign), Tier 2 (medium — blog, email, in-app), or Tier 3 (minor — changelog, docs update).
6. **What teams are involved?** Product, engineering, marketing, sales, support, legal — who has a deliverable?

If the user says "we're launching next month, write the brief," push back: "What is the core value proposition, and who specifically is the target audience?"

## Launch brief template

### 1. Launch Summary

- **Product/Feature**: [Name and one-line description]
- **Launch Date**: [Date, time, timezone]
- **Launch Tier**: [1 (major), 2 (medium), or 3 (minor)]
- **Launch Owner**: [Name and role]
- **One-Line Pitch**: [If a customer asks "what's new?" — this is the answer]

### 2. Positioning

Answer these three questions:

- **For** [target audience] **who** [current situation or pain point],
- **this** [product/feature] **provides** [key benefit],
- **unlike** [current alternative or competitor], **it** [key differentiator].

Write three versions: website, sales conversation, and internal alignment — same message at different detail levels.

### 3. Target Audience

| Segment | Description | Current Behavior | Desired Behavior | Key Message |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|------------------|-------------|
| Primary | [Who they are] | [What they do today] | [What you want them to do] | [Why they should switch] |
| Secondary | [Who they are] | [What they do today] | [What you want them to do] | [Why they should switch] |

### 4. Key Messages

Define 3-5 messages ranked by priority. Each needs:

1. **Message**: One sentence the audience should remember.
2. **Proof point**: Evidence (data, customer quote, demo).
3. **Objection**: Most likely pushback and response.

If everything is a key message, nothing is.

### 5. Launch Timeline

Work backward from the launch date:

| Date | Milestone | Owner |
|------|----------|-------|
| L-30 | Positioning and messaging approved | Product Marketing |
| L-14 | Blog post and landing page in review | Content + Design |
| L-7 | Sales enablement + press briefings | Sales Enablement + Comms |
| L-3 | In-app announcements configured | Product + Engineering |
| L-Day | Launch: press release, blog, email, social, in-app | All |
| L+7 | Post-launch review meeting | Launch Owner |

Adjust based on launch tier. Tier 3 launches may need only L-7 to L+1.

### 6. Channel Plan

| Channel | Asset | Owner | Date |
|---------|-------|-------|------|
| Blog | Launch announcement post | Content | L-Day |
| Email | Product update email | Email Marketing | L-Day |
| In-app | Feature announcement modal | Product | L-Day |
| Social | Thread with demo GIF | Social | L-Day |
| Sales | One-pager + talk track | Sales Enablement | L-21 |

For each channel, specify the CTA and target audience.

### 7. Success Metrics

| Metric | Target | Method | Timeframe |
|--------|--------|--------|-----------|
| Feature adoption rate | 15% of eligible users | Product analytics | 30 days |
| Blog post views | 5,000 | Web analytics | 7 days |
| Support ticket volume | < 2% increase | Support tool | 14 days |

Define what "success" and "underperformance" look like for each metric.

### 8. Cross-Functional Responsibilities

| Team | Deliverable | Due Date | Contact |
|------|------------|----------|---------|
| Product | Feature complete, feature flags ready | L-7 | [Name] |
| Engineering | Performance and scalability sign-off | L-7 | [Name] |
| Marketing | Blog, email, social assets | L-3 | [Name] |
| Sales | Trained on talk track and demo | L-7 | [Name] |
| Support | FAQ and escalation path documented | L-3 | [Name] |

Every row needs a named person, not a team alias. Accountability requires a single owner.

## Quality checklist

Before delivering the launch brief, verify:

- [ ] Positioning answers "for whom, what benefit, versus what alternative" in a single statement
- [ ] Target audience includes current behavior, desired behavior, and specific channel/CTA
- [ ] Key messages are limited to 3-5 with proof points and objection handling
- [ ] Timeline works backward from launch date with realistic lead times
- [ ] Every channel has an owner, publish date, and defined CTA
- [ ] Success metrics have numeric targets, measurement methods, and timeframes
- [ ] Every cross-functional deliverable has a single named owner and due date
- [ ] The brief fits on 2-3 pages — if it is longer, it will not be read

## Common mistakes to avoid

- **No positioning statement.** Jumping to channels and timelines without aligning on positioning means every team tells a different story. Finalize positioning first.
- **Too many messages.** Five key messages is the maximum. Beyond that, sales cannot remember them and customers hear noise.
- **Features instead of messages.** "We added SSO support" is a feature. "Sign in with one click using company credentials" is a message.
- **No post-launch plan.** Launch day is not the finish line. Define who monitors metrics and handles support spikes.
- **Unnamed owners.** "Marketing will handle the blog" assigns work to nobody. Every deliverable needs a named owner.
