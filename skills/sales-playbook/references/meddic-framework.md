# MEDDIC Qualification Framework

MEDDIC is a deal qualification methodology. It tells you whether a deal is real, winnable, and worth your time. Every element must have verifiable evidence — gut feelings and hopeful assumptions do not count.

---

## The Six Elements

### M — Metrics

**What it means:** The quantified business outcomes the buyer cares about. Not your metrics — theirs.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- "What business outcomes are you measured on this quarter?"
- "If you solve this problem, what changes in the numbers?"
- "How do you measure success for a project like this?"
- "What is the cost of the current problem — in time, revenue, or headcount?"

**Evidence required:** A specific number tied to their business. "Reduce onboarding time from 14 days to 3 days" is evidence. "Improve efficiency" is not.

**Failure mode:** The buyer says "we want to improve productivity" but cannot quantify it. If they cannot put a number on the problem, they cannot justify the budget. Coach them to quantify — or accept that this deal lacks urgency.

**Scoring:**
- **Strong (3):** Buyer has articulated specific, measurable outcomes with dollar values or time savings.
- **Medium (2):** Buyer has a general sense of impact but has not quantified it. You could help them build the business case.
- **Weak (1):** Buyer cannot articulate what success looks like. No quantified pain.

---

### E — Economic Buyer

**What it means:** The person who can approve the budget. Not the person who wants the product — the person who can sign the check.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- "Who approves budget for purchases at this price point?"
- "Have you bought anything in this price range in the past year? Who signed off?"
- "Is there a formal procurement process, or does [name] have authority to approve directly?"
- "Can we include [economic buyer name] in the next conversation?"

**Evidence required:** A name and title. You have confirmed (directly or through your champion) that this person has the authority and has been informed about the evaluation.

**Failure mode:** You are talking to an enthusiastic user who has zero budget authority. They love the product, promise to "bring it up with leadership," and the deal stalls for months. Always confirm: "Can [economic buyer] join our next call?"

**Scoring:**
- **Strong (3):** Economic buyer is identified by name, you have interacted with them directly, and they are engaged in the process.
- **Medium (2):** Economic buyer is identified by name and title, your champion confirms they are aware, but you have not spoken to them.
- **Weak (1):** You do not know who the economic buyer is, or your champion is vague ("I'll talk to my boss").

---

### D — Decision Criteria

**What it means:** The specific factors the buyer will use to evaluate and compare options. These are the requirements — spoken and unspoken — that determine who wins.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- "What are the must-have requirements for this solution?"
- "How will you compare options — is there a scorecard or evaluation matrix?"
- "Beyond features, what else matters? (Security, support, pricing model, implementation time)"
- "Are there any deal-breakers — things that would immediately disqualify a vendor?"

**Evidence required:** A list of criteria the buyer has shared, ideally ranked by importance. Even better: a formal evaluation scorecard or RFP.

**Failure mode:** You assume the decision criteria match your strengths. The buyer says "we care about ease of use" and you demo ease of use — but the actual decision hinges on SOC 2 compliance, which you only discover when the deal stalls in security review. Ask explicitly. Ask early.

**Scoring:**
- **Strong (3):** You have a documented list of criteria from the buyer, ranked by priority. You know where you win and where you are at risk.
- **Medium (2):** You have a general sense of criteria from conversations but nothing documented. Some criteria may be assumed.
- **Weak (1):** You are guessing at the decision criteria based on what similar buyers have cared about.

---

### D — Decision Process

**What it means:** The steps, people, and timeline required to go from "we like it" to "contract signed." This is the map of how the company buys.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- "What does the evaluation process look like from here?"
- "After a successful POC, what happens next — is there a security review, legal review, procurement?"
- "How long did the last purchase like this take from start to contract?"
- "Are there any approvals needed beyond [economic buyer]?"
- "Is there a specific date you need this in place by?"

**Evidence required:** A step-by-step process with approximate timelines. Example: "POC (2 weeks) > Security review (1 week) > Legal review (2 weeks) > Procurement (1 week) > Signature."

**Failure mode:** Your champion says "we should be able to move fast" — but their company has a 6-week procurement process that nobody mentioned. Map the process early, and validate it with someone who has been through it before (your champion or the economic buyer).

**Scoring:**
- **Strong (3):** You have a documented, step-by-step process with timelines and owners for each step. You have validated it with someone who has been through it.
- **Medium (2):** You have a rough outline of the process but are missing steps or timelines. Some assumptions.
- **Weak (1):** You do not know the process. Your champion says "it should be straightforward" but cannot describe the steps.

---

### I — Identify Pain

**What it means:** The specific, urgent problem the buyer needs to solve. Pain is the fuel that drives the deal — without it, nothing moves.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- "What is the biggest problem this solves for your team?"
- "What happens today when [problem scenario] occurs?"
- "How long has this been a problem? Why solve it now?"
- "What is the cost of not solving this in the next 6 months?"
- "Who else in the organization feels this pain?"

**Evidence required:** The buyer can describe the problem in specific terms, quantify its impact, and explain why it is urgent now. The pain should be widespread enough (multiple people affected) to justify the investment.

**Failure mode:** The buyer describes a mild inconvenience, not a real pain. "It would be nice to have" is not pain — it is a wish. Deals driven by wishes close at half the rate of deals driven by pain. If the pain is not acute, ask: "What would need to happen for this to become urgent?"

**Scoring:**
- **Strong (3):** Buyer describes specific, quantified pain that affects multiple people. There is a clear reason it must be solved now (trigger event, deadline, compounding cost).
- **Medium (2):** Buyer acknowledges the problem and can describe it, but has not quantified the impact or explained urgency.
- **Weak (1):** Buyer sees this as a "nice to have." No urgency, no quantified cost, no trigger event.

---

### C — Champion

**What it means:** An internal advocate who has power, influence, and a personal stake in your solution winning. They sell for you when you are not in the room.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- "Who on your team is most affected by this problem?"
- "Is there someone internally who has been pushing to solve this?"
- "Can you walk me through how you would present this to [economic buyer]?"
- "What would you need from me to make the internal case?"

**Evidence required:** Your champion can articulate why your product wins (not just that they like it). They have access to the economic buyer. They have a personal stake — their job gets easier, their team gets unblocked, or they get credit for solving a visible problem.

**Failure mode:** You mistake a friendly contact for a champion. A champion is not someone who takes your calls and says nice things. A champion actively sells for you internally — scheduling meetings, forwarding materials, and advocating in rooms you are not in. Test it: ask them to set up a meeting with the economic buyer. If they cannot or will not, they are a contact, not a champion.

**Scoring:**
- **Strong (3):** Champion has power/influence, articulates your value proposition, actively sells internally, and has set up meetings or shared materials on your behalf.
- **Medium (2):** Champion is supportive and has access to decision-makers, but has not yet taken visible action on your behalf.
- **Weak (1):** You have a friendly contact but no one actively advocating. Or your "champion" lacks influence or access to the economic buyer.

## Deal Scoring

Score each element 1-3. Total score: 6-18.

| Score Range | Assessment | Action |
|-------------|-----------|--------|
| 15-18 | Strong deal — high probability of closing | Accelerate. Invest time. Push for close timeline. |
| 11-14 | Viable but at risk — gaps need attention | Identify weakest elements. Build a plan to strengthen each. |
| 7-10 | Weak deal — significant qualification gaps | Decide: invest time to strengthen, or deprioritize and revisit later. |
| 6 | Unqualified — do not forecast this deal | Remove from pipeline or move to nurture. Do not spend selling time. |

**Rules:**
- Any element scored 1 is a deal risk regardless of total score. A deal with strong Metrics, Economic Buyer, Decision Criteria, Decision Process, and Pain (all 3s) but a Weak Champion (1) will stall.
- Re-score after every meaningful interaction. MEDDIC is not a one-time assessment.
- If you cannot score an element, that itself is a score of 1. Lack of information is not neutral — it is a risk.

## Using MEDDIC in Practice

### During Discovery Calls

Do not ask MEDDIC questions in order like a checklist. Weave them into conversation. "Walk me through how your team handles [process] today" covers Pain and Metrics. "Who else needs to be involved?" covers Economic Buyer and Decision Process.

### In Pipeline Reviews

For each deal in the pipeline, the rep should be able to answer:
1. What specific metrics justify this purchase?
2. Who is the economic buyer and have we engaged them?
3. What are their decision criteria, and where do we win/lose?
4. What are the remaining steps to a signed contract?
5. What is the quantified pain driving urgency?
6. Who is our champion, and what have they done to sell internally?

If the rep cannot answer any of these, the deal is not at the stage they think it is.

### When to Walk Away

Walk away (or deprioritize to nurture) when:
- There is no quantified pain and the buyer cannot articulate why this is urgent
- The economic buyer is unknown or inaccessible after multiple attempts
- Your champion has no influence or will not take action on your behalf
- The decision process has no timeline and no triggering event
- You score 7 or below and improvement is unlikely in the current quarter
